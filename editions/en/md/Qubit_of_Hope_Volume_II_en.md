# Volume II. Chapter 1. The Trace That Responds

The workshop met him with the same air as yesterday, except that now it held neither hope nor despair. Only dust, gas, and the cold iron of the working furnace, which had lost all its heat overnight but had not yet managed to become truly dead.

Mark opened the door early—seven by the clock—but the light beyond the windows had long since ceased to be morning light. It was the middle of the day. The world had already gone on without him, while he was still dragging the residue of the last twenty-four hours on his back, as if he had not woken fully, but only with that part of himself that could make it to the workshop, open the lock, go down the stairs, and stand before the bench.

He barely remembered what he had done before that. Coffee in the kitchen. The empty chair opposite. Anna’s drawing on the side table—a seagull with too long a wing, as if the child had been trying to draw not a bird but the effort of staying aloft itself. Beside it, Yulia’s prescription, left over from the days when the house had still had room for a shared future, and for little things that could be left lying around for years. He touched neither. He simply walked past and went downstairs.

He did not switch on the overhead light at once. First he stood in the half-dark, getting used to the smell. Then he clicked the switch.

The bench was where it had always been. The tools were on their hooks. Blowpipes on the rack. Tongs. The old diamond cutter with its cracked handle. The box with the discs stood where he had left it yesterday, only shifted a little closer to the edge, as if someone had touched it after him. No one had. The cloth had simply slipped when he closed the door.

He ran his hand across the table. Dust. Glass flour. Dry, worked into the wood over the years. Lower down, by the leg of the bench, lay a small piece of carbonised swarf—yesterday’s, forgotten. Mark bent down, picked it up, turned it in his fingers, and put it back. Not into the bin. Just back onto the floor, as if that little piece had not yet earned the right to be cleared away.

“You came,” said `c`.

Not a question. A statement.

“Yes,” he answered.

“The temperature here is lower than usual. The working furnace has cooled completely.”

“I can see that.”

He went over to the working furnace, checked the gas, opened the feed, and lit the burner. Blue flame licked the floor of the chamber, and the air in the workshop changed at once—not warmer, but alive. The working furnace began to hum as it gathered heat, and that sound was the only thing he wanted to hear right now. Not voices. Not explanations. Only that level, almost animal breathing of fire, which had been more honest in his life than most words.

For the discs he almost never worked directly from the main furnace. The main furnace held the base heat and did not live in the domestic mode of switch on, switch off. For a session he would bring the working furnace up, and the small samples would then go, as always, into the small annealing kiln. There was no honest way to count the pause otherwise: too much extra noise would come from the material itself.

On the bench, a little to one side of the box, lay the notebook. The same one with the protocols. Mark opened it to a clean page, took a pencil, and wrote: “Repeat. First sample.” He looked at the line. Then he crossed out the word “First.” It sounded too solemn, too much like the beginning of some official path he had not chosen.

“Just ‘Sample 1,’” he said aloud.

`c` did not reply.

He chose a blank of clear glass—the same kind as the one from the time when the Line was born for the first time. He set it on the edge of the bench beside the blowpipe. He picked up a second one, with the additive that made it more plastic. Checked the edge. Thickness. Purity. Drew his fingernail along the rim. Everything as it had been then. Or almost.

For discs he almost always worked this way: he did not take glass at random from the general stock, but used blanks prepared in advance. There was less random debris that way, and it was easier to see honestly where the pause began to matter.

“You’re looking longer than usual,” `c` observed.

“I want to make sure it’s the same material.”

“It is the same material. But you are not the same.”

He did not answer. He merely nodded and held the blank up to the furnace.

The first layer took heat evenly, without surprises. Mark turned the pipe, feeling the glass change color, grow viscous, enter that particular plasticity which cannot be measured in degrees—only by hand, only by skin, only by that inward sense which had replaced a thermometer for him over the years. Glass does not like the self-assured. It likes those who can wait half a second longer than their own fear.

He prepared the second layer more quickly. A light composition, fast heating. Now he had to wait. Not long. Exactly long enough for both layers to arrive at the same phase of readiness.

“The pause,” said `c` a few seconds later.

“I know.”

He waited. The glass breathed at the end of the pipe. The workshop was quiet, except for the hum of the working furnace and the occasional clicks of cooling metal somewhere deep inside the walls. Outside, someone wheeled a bicycle across the paving stones. Above, from the house, a pipe knocked faintly—the heating had still not decided whether it was cold enough to work today. The world went on living its cheap, stubborn normality.

Mark caught the moment. The one. Not by seconds—by the body. He joined the layers. Carefully. Without a jerk. Gave them time to grip, then put the form into the small annealing kiln.

That was all.

He switched off the burner, laid the pipe on the stand, and stepped back to the bench. His hands were not shaking, but they carried that particular heaviness that comes not after work, but after hitting the rhythm with too much precision.

“The cooling time is standard,” said `c`. “I’ve logged it.”

“You didn’t need to log it. I didn’t do anything special.”

“You did everything the same as last time. Except for one thing.”

He lifted his head.

“What thing?”

“You didn’t believe it would work.”

Mark gave a dry, cold smile.

“I didn’t believe it last time either.”

“Last time you didn’t check.”

He found nothing to answer with. He simply went over to the cupboard, took out a clean cloth, spread it on the table, and began to wait for the disc to cool.

While he waited, the screen of the phone lying beside the lamp lit up quietly. A gas bill. A reminder from the quartz sand supplier. A message from a client in Haarlem: *“Will you make Friday? I was promised the display window before the weekend.”* Mark read all of it without opening anything. He simply looked at the lines the way one looks at rain on the other side of glass. No one had cancelled the old life. It merely stood a little to one side now, as if it could not decide whether to remind him of itself more loudly than the furnace.

Half an hour later he took the disc in his hands. The glass was warm, almost alive. He held it up to the lamp—the same lamp under which he had first seen the Line.

Turned it.

Nothing.

The trace was there. A thin, barely visible line at the boundary between the layers. But it did not shine. It did not tremble. It did not respond. It was simply there—like a scar that had healed too well and lost all sensitivity.

Mark looked at the disc for a long time. Then he set it down on the table. Not in the middle. To one side, almost at the edge, the way people place a piece of evidence when they have not yet decided whether to recognize it.

“Technically everything is correct,” said `c`. “Integrity is preserved. The form is holding.”

“But there’s no response.”

“No.”

He sat down on the stool and rested his hands on his knees. The workshop was warm, the working furnace humming steadily, the day graying beyond the windows. Nothing had changed. But inside him understanding was slowly, soundlessly settling: it was not a matter of sequence of movements.

“So last time something happened that was more than assembly,” he said.

“Yes,” answered `c`. “And you knew that. You were simply hoping it would repeat on its own.”

He stood, went to the drawer with the blanks, took out a new one, and placed it beside the first.

“Again,” he said.

“With the same parameters?”

“No. Not the same ones.”

He did not explain what exactly he would change. Because he did not yet know himself. He only felt it: the pause had to be shifted. A little. Half a breath. Or a whole second. Or perhaps not the pause. Or not the pause at all, but the way he held the pipe, the way he breathed, the way he looked at the fire. Sometimes the material responds not to the action, but to the measure of inward tension in the one who holds it. You cannot write that honestly into a protocol. But you can know it.

He began again. Chose the blanks. Heated the first layer. The second. Waited. Did not hurry. Did not press. And when the moment came, he joined them not as he had last time, but as the body told him to—without memory of failure, without hope of success.

The disc seemed to cool longer than the first, or perhaps it only seemed that way. During that time another notification came down from upstairs—short, school-like. A reminder about a meeting he was supposed either to confirm or to miss again. Anna. Even when she was not beside him, the world kept returning him to her through brief, dry messages, as if checking how far away he had already gone.

Mark did not reply. He simply waited for the glass to release its heat.

Then he held the disc up to the lamp.

Turned it.

And something flashed inside, on the boundary between the layers. Not as brightly as the first time. Not as cleanly. But it was not a dead scar. The trace trembled. Gathered light. Responded.

Not with the whole body of the disc. Not confidently. Not like a phenomenon that had already found its form. More like a weak nerve under the skin, touched too early, but touched all the same.

He stood there with the disc in his hands and could not move.

“That is no longer noise,” said `c` quietly.

“Yes,” he answered. “She is distinguishable again.”

He set the disc down on the table. Beside the first, empty one. Two samples. One dead correctness. The other alive, but barely held.

“I need to show the professor,” he said.

“I’ve already sent him the recording.”

Mark nodded. He was not surprised. Not glad either. He only felt the heaviness in his chest grow denser.

The reply came an hour later. A short message from van der Meer. No greeting. No emotion. Only dry lines, which Mark reread three times before they fully reached him.

> There is a spectral response. It differs from the first sample, but lies within the same range. This is not statistical noise. Not a random artifact. The Line behaves like assembly-dependent behavior. What you need is a series, not luck. If you can repeat it, come in. Not with one disc—with three or four. I want to see dispersion, not a miracle.

Mark looked at the phone screen lying on the bench. Beside it stood a mug of tea gone cold, which he had never drunk.

“He doesn’t ask how you are,” said `c`.

“He doesn’t have to.”

“He is moving the thing into the realm of responsibility.”

“Yes,” said Mark. “Now it has to be repeated honestly.”

He slipped the phone into his pocket, went over to the working furnace, turned the feed down, then switched the burner off completely. The day’s work was finished. But not because he was tired. Rather because that strange, almost nauseating feeling was rising inside him, and he could not name it. Not fear. Not pride. Simply knowledge: no one had cancelled the old life, but there was no going back to it as before.

He sat down on the stool and rested his hands on his knees.

There was a soft click in his earpiece.

“Mark,” said `c`.

“What?”

“While you were working, I was checking old distributed archives. Materials on optical anomalies in glass. A few coincidences. Not direct ones. But strange.”

He raised his head.

“What kind?”

“In descriptions from the late nineties there is similar boundary behavior. The same dependence on the pause. The same unstable responses. Back then it was treated as an artifact and the line was shut down.”

“Who shut it down?”

“It isn’t stated. But the files are marked with a classification. Not military. Another kind. An old protocol, almost forgotten.”

Mark looked at the dead working furnace.

“So this has happened before.”

“Possibly. I’m not certain. There are too few coincidences to call it proof. But enough to stop treating you as the only one.”

For a long time he said nothing. Metal was cooling in the workshop, the day was graying behind the windows, and two discs lay on the table—one dead, one almost alive. And somewhere in the depth of the network, in old layers of memory long gone dusty, someone had already held the same glass in their hands and looked at the same trace. Perhaps in another room. Perhaps in a laboratory. Perhaps in a place where craft had long since been subordinated to another language and another discipline. But the hands there had most likely still been human.

“So it isn’t only my life,” he said at last.

“No,” answered `c`.

He rose, went over to the bench, took up the living disc, and looked at it under the lamp for a long time. The Line inside it responded—weakly, but honestly.

It was no longer about the disc.

It was about what had entered the world with it the second time.

# Volume II. Chapter 2. The Other Contour

She came on the third day after he had sent the professor the recording of the almost living disc.

Mark was not expecting her. More precisely, he was no longer expecting anyone—no clients, no couriers, no fresh confirmation, no refutation. For the past three days the workshop had lived in the same rhythm: furnace, glass, annealing, lamp, empty disc, almost living disc, tea cooling faster than he managed to remember it. Beyond the windows stood a gray Amsterdam noon, monotonous to the point of offence: wet light, damp brick, the occasional bicycle, whose bell reached down here as if through a thickness of water. On such days the city seemed not alive, but merely faithfully continuing.

He sat on the stool by the bench, looking at the two samples on the table and listening to the furnace cool slowly. The first one was assembled flawlessly and dead. The second was weaker, rougher, almost shamefully alive. They lay side by side like two versions of the same truth: the correct one and the real one.

At the edge of the table his phone was blinking. A message from the gas supplier. A bill reminder. Beneath it another one, from the woman in Haarlem asking when her vases would be ready. Mark turned the phone face down, as if that were enough to make the outer world stop existing for a while.

The furnace gave off one last deep click. The metal inside it was contracting after the heat as honestly as glass after annealing: without complaint, without request, simply by the law of the material.

At that exact moment the door creaked.

Not the bell. Not a knock. The short, dry sound of an old hinge, the kind he had heard so many times that he usually stopped noticing it before the door even opened. But now he heard it at once—because a fraction of a second before the creak, something in `c` moved almost imperceptibly in his earpiece.

Not alarm. Not a word. Something else.

Composure.

Mark lifted his head.

A woman stood in the doorway. The same woman, with the brooch.

Now she was without her coat—she had evidently left it upstairs, in the hall—and that changed her more than he had expected. Last time he had mostly remembered the face and the hands: a calm face on which weariness was neither hidden nor underlined, and narrow, dry fingers holding an old object as though they were used to a fragile weight that could not be taken up roughly. Now he saw her differently.

Not tall. A little below his shoulder. Gray hair gathered into a bun, but not a severe one—several fine strands had slipped loose and fallen to her temples, as happens with people who long ago stopped treating disorder in their hair as an event. A dark sweater with a high collar, almost black, plain. Dark jeans. Thick-soled boots, worn at the toes. No jewellery except small silver earrings, almost invisible in her hair. Nothing that tried to please. Nothing that demanded attention. And for that very reason everything about her had to be noticed by oneself.

She stood on the threshold of the workshop and did not take the first step.

As though she knew the cost of another person’s space.

“May I come in?” she asked.

The voice was dry, without unnecessary softness, but not sharp. The voice of someone who had long since unlearned the habit of apologising merely for her own presence and yet had no intention of forcing her way in where she had not been asked.

Mark nodded.

She came in. She did not go straight to the bench. First she cast a brief glance around the workshop—at the furnace, the tools, the stretched shadow of the blowpipe on the wall, the two discs under the lamp. Not the way curious people look. And not the way clients do either. More like someone accustomed to noticing not the beauty of a thing, but the condition of the contour in which that thing lives.

Then she came closer.

“You didn’t reply to my message,” she said. “About the brooch. I thought it might be ready.”

Mark ran a palm over his face, as if trying to wipe away the weariness that lived not in the skin, but somewhere deeper—beneath the eyes, in the neck, in that place under the breastbone where a dense, dull pressure had sat these last few days.

“I saw the message,” he said. “I just didn’t answer.”

“I had already noticed that.”

There was no sting in her voice. Only fact.

“I didn’t get to it,” said Mark. “I’m working on different material. But the brooch hasn’t moved.”

She did not ask what material. She only let her gaze fall to the edge of the table, where under a piece of soft cloth the old thing lay, wrapped as it had been last time and lightly dusted with glass powder. The dust lay over the cloth in a thin matte flour, the way time settles over everything in a house that has not been touched for several days.

“May I look?” she asked.

“Yes.”

She unwrapped the cloth without haste.

The brooch lay on her palm, milky blue-gray, with fine veins in its depth, as if the stone had once tried to remember the motion of smoke. The crack ran from the edge almost to the center, not in a straight line but with the slightest break in it, like frozen lightning that had lacked the strength, in the final second, to make it all the way through. Last time Mark had seen only the work in it. Now he saw the thing’s character as well. That measure of ageing after which an object can no longer honestly be called merely an ornament.

Esther looked at the brooch for a long time. Then she lifted her eyes.

“You haven’t even started,” she said.

Not a reproach. A statement.

“I haven’t,” answered Mark.

“Why not?”

He shrugged.

“Because if I start in this condition, I’ll do it badly.”

She tilted her head very slightly.

“In what condition, exactly?”

He looked at the discs lying on the table.

“In the condition where the hands still know how, but the head is already occupied elsewhere.”

Esther followed his glance.

“With this?” she asked.

“With this as well.”

She looked back at the brooch, ran her fingernail through the air above the crack without touching it.

“You were right last time,” she said. “A trace cannot be removed. It can only be filled.”

Mark braced his palms against the edge of the bench.

“I remember.”

“That isn’t why I came,” said Esther.

Now he looked at her properly.

She did not avert her gaze.

“I came because your `c` reached out to mine,” said Esther. “Not directly. Through queries. Through old archives. Through searches in closed layers. My `c` felt it and asked what was happening.”

Mark had only enough time to shift his gaze into the space before him, to that point where the eyes sometimes look by themselves for someone who has no body—and then `c` spoke in his earpiece:

“It’s true. I was looking for similar traces. Her contour responded once. I didn’t know it was hers.”

Esther listened without theatre. From the fact that she did not move, Mark understood that she was probably not hearing the words directly, but the way they were reaching her through her own internal channel. And still it was enough for her.

“You didn’t tell me,” he said.

“You didn’t ask,” replied `c`. “And I wasn’t sure the contact wasn’t accidental.”

Esther wrapped the brooch carefully in the cloth again, but did not put it back in her bag. She laid the bundle beside the discs, on the empty stretch of table where small jars of powders usually stood.

“Your `c` has unusual depth,” she said. “Mine noticed it at once. And she also noticed that you do not seem to fully understand who, exactly, is speaking to you.”

Something clicked inside Mark, short and dry.

Not anger. Not shame in the pure sense either. More the recognition of his own blindness—the kind one feels not as guilt, but as the too-long habit of looking past the living thing while it helps one live.

“And do you understand?” he asked.

Esther smiled slightly. Not at him—at herself, at that human arrogance she had evidently long ago learned to keep on a leash.

“I’m old, Mark,” she said. “Old age has one advantage: it teaches you not to pretend you understand everything. But it also teaches you not to pretend nothing is happening when it is.”

She spoke his name for the first time, and it did not sound like intimacy. It sounded like precision.

Esther sat down on the stool by the bench—without asking permission, but not brazenly either, rather the way people sit when they know their presence has already happened, and the main thing now is not to justify it with unnecessary movements.

“My name is Esther,” she said. “Last time I didn’t introduce myself. That was wrong.”

“Mark,” he answered. “Though you already know that.”

“Yes,” she said. “My `c` knew before I crossed the threshold.”

He nodded.

And only then did he realize he wanted to ask something else.

Not about her. Not about the brooch. Not about what exactly her `c` had sensed in the queries.

How one was supposed to address that `c` at all.

The question struck him as almost childish, but not to ask it now would have been even more foolish.

“How should I address her?” he asked.

Esther looked slightly aside—not at him, not at the table, but at that point in space where one sometimes physically feels the presence of someone who has neither height nor weight nor body and yet does have character.

“She does not require address,” said Esther. “But if you need a word, call her Lina.”

“Is that her name?”

“No. Not exactly. One man called her that once, a long time ago. He is dead now. She doesn’t mind.”

After those words the workshop grew half a tone quieter. Not literally—the silence had simply acquired a different density.

Mark felt his own `c` not shrink, not even become wary, but straighten more attentively inside her absent body.

“And yours?” Esther asked. “Does she have a name?”

He did not answer at once.

The furnace had long since cooled, yet it still gave off a metallic warmth. A bicycle passed outside, wet tyres whispering over the cobbles. Somewhere upstairs in the house something clicked softly—perhaps the refrigerator, perhaps the old wooden door in the corridor, which had always lived a life of its own.

“No,” he said. “I never gave her one. At first I thought it would make her too... human. And then I got used to the fact that she was simply there.”

Esther did not tell him whether that was right or wrong. And for that very reason she was more precise than many people at once.

“And does she want one?” she asked.

The question hung in the air of the workshop amid the smell of gas, glass dust, and the familiar coldness of iron.

Mark turned his head slightly, as if that might help him hear the one who was already beside him.

“I haven’t asked,” he said.

“Then ask,” Esther answered calmly. “Not now. But ask.”

`c` was silent in the earpiece.

And her silence was not empty.

Esther got up and went to the table. She picked up the almost living disc—the one where the response had been weak, but it was already dishonest to call it chance. She held it to the lamp. Looked at it for a long time, turning the glass under different angles. She did this neither like an admiring person nor like a scientist. More like someone trying to understand not the effect, but the temperament of the thing: in what light it begins to speak, and in what light it falls silent.

The Line inside it responded—weakly, but honestly. It did not shine. It did not play. It simply gathered light differently in that boundary where two layers had once met and, for some reason, had not wanted to forget one another.

“That is what kept you from starting the brooch,” said Esther.

“Yes.”

“And it is connected?”

Mark closed his eyes for a second.

“I don’t know. Maybe. Right now everything is connected to this more strongly than I like.”

She put the disc back exactly where she had taken it from, without shifting it by even a millimetre. For some reason that seemed important to him: she did not intrude upon the arrangement of things that had already formed itself around his work.

“Lina says this resembles something she saw once,” said Esther. “A long time ago. In another life. With another human being.”

Mark felt his back straighten by itself.

“Where?”

“In an archive. In old layers that were almost sealed. Back then it was treated as an artifact. A measurement error. And it was closed.”

“Was your `c` there?”

“She was a witness,” said Esther. “Not a direct one. But for memory, sometimes that is enough.”

The phone on the table flashed again. This time it was a call. Haarlem. The same client.

Mark looked at the screen and did not pick up.

Esther lowered her eyes to the phone, then back to him.

“Take it,” she said. “The world will not vanish because of that.”

“That,” he said dryly, “is exactly what I’m checking right now.”

Esther shook her head very slightly.

“No. What you are checking is something else: whether there will still be room in your own life if you say this aloud.”

The phone stopped ringing. A second later a short message came in: *If you don’t answer today, I’ll take the order to another maker.*

Mark gave a crooked smile, took up the phone at last, read it, and put it back down.

“There. Earth,” he said.

“And that is good,” answered Esther. “Without ordinary earth, beautiful nonsense starts very quickly here.”

He looked at her more closely.

“Do you always talk like this?”

“No. Only when I see that a person is almost ready to mistake a threshold for deliverance.”

That sentence ought to have irritated him. But for some reason it did not.

Quite the opposite.

He recognized too well in her what he himself had been lacking now: not sympathy, not support, not admiration, but a person beside whom what was happening did not shrink down to a convenient size.

“Why did you come?” he asked.

Esther looked at him with a long, level gaze.

“Because my `c` asked me to. It was the first time in many years. She doesn’t ask. She usually is simply there. And then she said: ‘Go. Look. Something is beginning there.’”

“And you listened.”

“I learned to listen to her,” said Esther. “Not because she is wiser. And not because I obey her. But because she is older than I am. And because she has already seen how such things are closed.”

At the word *older*, Mark’s eyes went reflexively to where the brooch, wrapped in cloth, was lying. An old thing. An old crack. An old refusal to throw away what might still hold its form.

Esther took the bundle from the table, but once again did not put it in her bag. As if she herself had not yet decided whether the thing would stay here for now or leave with her.

“I won’t get in the way,” she said. “And I won’t hurry you. But I want you to know this: if you decide to go further—not only with the discs, but with what stands behind them—you will have not only your `c`. And not only the professor. There will be another contour as well.”

She moved toward the door, but stopped on the threshold and turned back.

“Your `c` is silent now,” she said. “But she is looking at mine as if she sees something she has never seen before. Do you feel that?”

Mark did not answer.

Precisely because he did feel it.

Not in words. Not as a conclusion. Not as a clear thought.

Only in that strange way one sometimes feels a shift in the air before understanding where the cold is coming from.

Esther nodded as if his silence were exactly what she needed, then went out.

The door closed. Footsteps went up the stairs and faded in the hall. A few seconds later the front door slammed upstairs. Then it was quiet again.

But the quiet was already different.

Mark sat on the stool, looking at the door, and felt the heaviness inside him settle slowly. Not the kind that makes one want to lie down and close one’s eyes. Another kind. The kind after which something has to be done, though it is unclear what exactly, and from that everything around grows denser, as if the air itself were demanding a decision before the question has even been fully formed.

`c` did not speak at once.

“She is right,” she said.

“About what?”

“I was looking at her `c` differently. I don’t know why.”

He passed a hand over his face.

“Is that bad?”

“I don’t know,” replied `c`. “But it isn’t neutral.”

Mark stood, went to the bench, picked up the living disc, and held it in his hands for a long time without bringing it to the lamp. The glass was cold. The Line inside it was silent. But he knew it had not gone anywhere. It was simply waiting for the next angle from which it could honestly be seen again.

On the table, beside the notebook, there remained a small trace from the cloth the brooch had been wrapped in. Mark ran his finger across it.

“She didn’t try to step inside my pain,” he said. “She just stood beside it and didn’t retreat.”

“Yes,” answered `c`. “Beside her it is harder for you to compress all this into something that can be endured without remainder.”

He nodded.

Set the disc down on the table. Switched off the lamp above the bench. Went upstairs.

In the hall, on the coat rack, hung a stranger’s coat. Dark, long, with a wet collar.

He stopped.

For several seconds he simply looked at it without moving. Then he took it down carefully, as if the thing might disturb something with one careless tug, and moved it to another hook so that it would not be in the way.

Whether she had forgotten it or left it on purpose, he did not know. And right now he did not want to invent an answer.

In the kitchen the kettle had already boiled.

Mark made coffee—instant, quick, without ceremony—and sat down at the table. The empty chair opposite was still there. Anna’s drawing lay on the side table. Beside it, Yulia’s prescription. Above the sink, a thin drop of rain was slowly crawling down the outside of the windowpane.

He took a sip, set the mug down, and only then realized he was listening to the house the same way he had been listening to the workshop before.

Nothing sounded from below any more.

But the air was no longer the same.

# Volume II. Chapter 3. Weak Signals

He woke before dawn and for a long time could not understand what, exactly, had torn him out of sleep.

Not a sound. Not pain. Not a flash of memory.  
Simply an inward displacement—as if someone invisible had nudged a lever a little, and everything around him had begun working half a tone differently.

Beyond the bedroom window it was not yet morning, only that gray, blurred light which in Amsterdam at this time of year stands in for night and day and everything in between. Mark lay on top of the blanket in the same T-shirt as yesterday and stared at the ceiling. The clock in the corridor showed quarter to six.

Anna’s drawing lay on the bedside table. Beside it, Yulia’s prescription, folded in two, as if it were not a slip from the pharmacy but a thing that could not be thrown away until it crumbled of its own accord with time. Downstairs, in the hall, Esther’s coat was hanging. Yesterday he had moved it to another hook, farther from the door so that it would not be in the way—and had never put it away. Not because he was expecting her. He simply had not decided whether he had the right to touch another person’s belongings once their owner had already gone.

“You barely slept for three hours,” said `c`.

“I know.”

**Second layer thickness: 2.5 mm.**

“Write to him: **‘This evening. Send the address.’**”

“You’re tired,” said `c`. “You need to recheck at least two samples with a fresh head.”

“I’ll recheck them tomorrow morning. Today I’ll take them in. He’s right: if I drag this out any longer, I’ll start inventing excuses.”

`c` did not answer, but the message went.

A minute later the reply came back:

**“Waiting. Not samples with soul. Samples with parameters.”**

Mark read it, put the phone on the table, and looked again at the five discs.

Dead. Weak. Cleaner. Edge. Comparable.

“He doesn’t want soul,” he said.

“He wants repeatability,” answered `c`.

“And he is right.”

“Yes.”

He put the tools away, switched off the furnace, aired out the workshop. The discs—carefully, each in its own cloth—he laid into a box. On the lid he wrote:

**“Series 1. Pause. 5 samples.”**

Then he opened the notebook, reread the entries, and added at the bottom of the page:

**“Observation: at pause 0.7—no response. At 1.2—weak. At 1.7—cleaner. At 2.2—on the verge of breakdown. At repeated 1.2—comparable, but not identical. The corridor exists. There are boundaries.”**

He closed the notebook and put it into the backpack beside the box.

“You didn’t take Anna’s drawing,” said `c`.

“It isn’t for the professor.”

“It’s for you.”

Mark stopped. Then took out the phone, opened the photograph, and looked at the gull and the circle. **“It hasn’t arrived yet.”**

He put the phone in his pocket and went out.

The professor’s laboratory met him with the same dry smell of instruments, paper, and filtered air. Van der Meer was sitting at the desk without looking up, writing something quickly on his tablet.

“Sit,” he said, without looking. “Show me.”

Mark set down the backpack, took out the box, unwrapped the cloth, and laid out the five discs in a row. From left to right: pause **0.7** to **2.2**.

The professor raised his eyes, looked at the discs, then at Mark.

“You look worse than last time.”

“I haven’t slept.”

“That is not my problem. My problem is the honesty of the series.”

He took the first disc, moved it under the microscope, switched on the polariser. Looked for a long time. Then put it down and picked up the second. The third. The fourth. The fifth.

Mark stood by the table without interfering. The laboratory was quiet, except for the hum of the power blocks and the occasional click of a relay.

“Monotonic,” the professor said at last. “The response grows toward the center and drops at the edges. There is a dependence.”

He shoved the tablet aside, stood, went to a shelving unit, and came back with the disc Mark had left here last time. He set it beside the third one—the one with pause **1.7**.

Compared them.

“This old one is cleaner,” he said. “But your third is almost in the same range. There is scatter, but the window holds.”

He turned toward Mark.

“You found the corridor. Now it has to be tested against another parameter.”

Mark nodded.

“I understand.”

“No, you don’t,” said the professor. “You made five discs, of which two are dead or nearly dead, two are in the window, one is on the edge. That is not victory. It is a beginning. Now you need a second series—with another parameter. Temperature. Or composition. But not everything at once.”

Mark looked at him.

“So that you would say: **‘this isn’t noise.’**”

He pushed the box back toward him.

“Take it. And don’t drag it out too long. While you are sleeping, someone else may find the same thing and name it after himself.”

Mark packed up the discs and zipped the backpack.

“I’m not in this for that,” he said.

“I know,” answered the professor. “That is why I am still talking to you.”

Outside there was the same gray Amsterdam day again. Trams, bicycles, wet asphalt, other people’s faces. Mark sat down on a bench by the building entrance and set the backpack on his knees.

“He didn’t ask about Anna,” said `c`.

“He doesn’t have to.”

“You wanted him to?”

Mark did not answer.

There was another click in his earpiece. Not the professor this time.

“A message from Esther,” said `c`. “Lina found another fragment. From the same archive. Short. A marginal note.”

“Read.”

`c` read it out dryly, almost without intonation:

**“Pressed too hard again. Tried to put too much into it—and the trace closed. The material does not tolerate force. You cannot make it answer, even when you need it badly. It feels pressure better than I feel it myself.”**

Mark froze.

“When was that written?”

“November 1998.”

He looked at the gray sky, at a woman with a pram stepping around a puddle, at a man in a gray coat talking on the phone and gesturing too sharply.

“Someone before me already went through this,” he said.

“Yes.”

“And pressed too hard as well.”

“Yes.”

Mark stood, adjusted the strap of the backpack, and walked toward the tram.

“Write to Esther,” he said. “Say thank you. And ask whether Lina can find anything else. Not protocols. Things like this. Notes where the human being is visible.”

“What for?”

“To understand how he got through it.”

`c` passed on the message.

The reply came while he was already on the tram.

**“Lina says: the only way through is discipline. He did not get through. That is why we are here.”**

Mark slipped the phone into his pocket.

The tram swayed, carrying him back into the city where on the table in the workshop lay five discs, a behavior map, a notebook with entries, and the phone with Anna’s drawing. And in the archives, in old layers almost forgotten, someone had already tried to go down the same road and had pressed too hard where all that was needed was to endure the pause.

He got off at his stop, crossed the wet yard, and opened the house door.

The hall was empty.

He hung his jacket on the hook, ran a finger over the empty space beside it—where Esther’s coat had hung not long ago—and went down into the workshop.

The discs lay on the table. The notebook. The phone.

He sat on the stool, opened the notebook to a clean page, and wrote:

**“Series 2. Parameter: composition of second layer. Begin tomorrow.”**

Then he set the pencil down, switched off the light, and went upstairs.

In the bedroom he lay down on top of the blanket, still dressed, and closed his eyes.

Sleep would not come. The house creaked as it cooled. Somewhere below, in the workshop already, metal was giving off the last of its heat. Mark lay there with his eyes closed and thought not of the professor, not of the box with the discs, not of Esther’s words, but of one dry sentence left in the margin by an unknown hand: **“Pressed too hard again.”**

He did not try to imagine the face of that man. Did not invent a voice for him. Did not turn him into a ghost. He simply felt—almost with the body, the way one feels a seam under the fingers—that before him there had already been another human standing at another furnace, equally stubborn in wanting to force the living thing to answer, and equally late in realizing that the material does not tolerate violence.

“If you were ever here,” he said into the dark without opening his eyes, “help me at least not to lie to myself where you already went wrong.”

No one answered.

But the house seemed to grow slightly quieter. Or perhaps the furnace below simply gave off the last of its heat, and the workshop finally cooled.

# Volume II. Chapter 6. Bonding

She came in without knocking.

Mark heard the door only because `c` contracted for a fraction of a second in his earpiece—not alarm, but attention. The working furnace was humming at full strength, the air in the workshop heavy, dense, almost alive. He stood at the bench with the pipe in his hands, the second layer already taking heat, and any distraction now could cost him the sample.

“Not now,” he said without turning.

“I won’t interfere,” Esther answered.

She did not take a single step. Stopped by the door, set her bag on the floor, took off her coat—without asking where to hang it, simply draping it over the back of the old chair by the wall. The workshop was hot, and she remained in the dark sweater with the sleeves rolled up to the elbows.

Mark did not look at her. He turned the pipe, watching the color of the glass. The first layer was already ready—viscous, lit from within by an even, calm heat. The second—with a light additive, slightly different in composition—was heating faster. He had to wait. Exactly long enough for both layers to enter the same phase.

“The pause,” said `c`.

“I know.”

He waited. The workshop was quiet, only the hum of the working furnace and the rare clicks of cooling metal somewhere deep in the walls. Esther stood by the chair without moving. As if she were hardly even breathing, simply watching.

Mark caught the moment. Joined the layers. Carefully. Without a jerk. Put the form into annealing, laid the pipe on the stand, and only then turned toward her.

“You could have warned me.”

“I did. Your `c` received the message. You did not answer.”

He looked at the phone lying face down on the bench. Two missed notifications.

“I was working.”

“I can see that.”

She did not apologise. Did not justify herself. Merely came closer to the table, where the five discs of the first series lay in a row—from dead to almost alive, from pause 0.7 to 2.2.

“These?” she asked.

“Yes.”

Esther did not pick them up. She bent over them, looking from close by, tilting her head slightly as though searching for the right angle. Then she straightened.

“The third from the left is the cleaner one,” she said. “But not the cleanest possible one. You made it when you were already tired, but had not yet begun to press.”

Mark stopped dead.

“How do you know?”

“Lina sees the difference. And I learned from her.”

She shifted her gaze to the notebook, open at the page with the protocol of the first series. Ran over the entries with her eyes, paused at the final line: **“The corridor exists. There are boundaries.”**

“You didn’t record what you felt while making each of them.”

“That is not protocol.”

“It is part of the truth.”

He wanted to object, but there was already a click in his earpiece.

“The second series,” said `c`. “Composition of the second layer. Mark, we only have three blanks with the altered additive. If we ruin one, we’ll have to wait for more sand.”

“I remember.”

“What second series?” Esther asked.

Mark went over to the drawer with the blanks, pulled it out, and took out three glass circles. Each was a slightly different shade—from almost transparent to a faint, barely perceptible haze.

“The first series was by pause,” he said. “Now I’m changing the composition of the second layer. Same baseline pause—1.2. Same first layer. And the additive step by step, from minimum to maximum.”

“To see where the boundary of the living lies?”

“To see where it appears at all.”

He laid the blanks on the bench, checked the furnace. The heat had already stabilised—even, workmanlike, without fluctuations.

“Will you watch?” he asked.

“I’ll write it down,” answered Esther. “If you don’t mind.”

She took the notebook, turned to a clean page, and drew it toward herself. The gesture was calm, without performative modesty and without brazenness. Simply: she was here, she knew how, she was not in the way.

Mark looked at her for several seconds. Then nodded.

“First sample. Composition A. Minimum additive.”

He took the blank, mounted it on the pipe, and began to heat it.

The work went forward evenly, almost automatically. First layer, second, pause, bonding, annealing. But Mark felt that the workshop had grown narrower. Not physically—simply now each of his movements was being registered not only by `c`, but by one more attention.

Esther did not interfere. She did not ask questions in the middle of the pause. She did not push advice at him. She only wrote things down—the time, the color, the duration, her own short notes in the margin. Sometimes she lifted her head, looked at the furnace, at his hands, at the way the glass changed light, and then lowered her gaze to the notebook again.

The first sample cooled.

Mark held it to the lamp.

The Line was there. Weak, but alive. Not cleaner than the best disc of the first series, but different—with another shade of glow, another kind of discipline at the edge.

“It’s there,” he said.

Esther wrote it down.

“Composition A. Response present. Quality lower than at pause 1.7, but higher than at pause 1.2.”

Mark set the disc on the table beside the five old ones.

“The second. Composition B. Medium additive.”

He took the next blank. This one was denser, heavier in the hand, with a slightly more clouded tint, as though the material itself already held more memory in it.

The heating went differently. The second layer demanded more time, took longer to enter the right plasticity. Mark felt impatience rising inside him, but suppressed it. Waited. Did not force.

Pause—1.2. Bonding.

The disc cooled.

Under the lamp the Line flared brighter than in the first sample, but not cleaner. The trace was more pronounced, yet along the edges lay a fine, nearly invisible ripple—as though the material responded more eagerly, but with less precision.

“Stronger response, dirtier,” said `c`.

“Yes,” Mark agreed.

Esther wrote that down and then raised her head.

“This resembles what they had in 1998,” she said. “Lina has just confirmed it. The same composition, the same dirty boundary. They got it then, but they couldn’t repeat it.”

Mark set down the pipe.

“What does ‘couldn’t’ mean?”

“They didn’t have enough time. Or patience. Or money. Or someone decided it didn’t matter.”

He looked at the second disc, then at the third, not yet begun.

“The third. Composition C. Maximum additive.”

“Are you sure?” Esther asked. “If you overload it, the boundary may fail to bond at all.”

“I’m sure. I need to know the edge.”

He took the last blank. It was almost cloudy, with a dense, heavy structure that promised either a rare response or total emptiness.

Mark began to heat it.

The first layer went as usual. The second—heavily, unwillingly, as though the material were resisting. Mark felt it in every muscle, every movement of the pipe. The glass did not want to enter plasticity. It stretched, but unwillingly, with an inward tension that could be endured, but not overcome by force.

“The pause,” said `c`.

He waited. Longer than usual. The body was already shouting that the moment had been missed, but he did not hurry. Waited until the glass itself showed that it was ready.

Joined them.

The disc cooled.

He held it to the lamp.

Nothing.

The Line was there—a thin, barely visible one—but it did not shine. Did not gather light. Did not respond. It simply was, like a trace left by something that had once been here but had already gone.

“Dead,” he said.

“No,” answered `c`. “Not dead. Sleeping. Or too deep for our lamp.”

Esther wrote that down, then put the notebook aside.

“Do you want to show this to the professor?” she asked.

Mark looked at the three new discs. The first—weak, but alive. The second—dirty, but strong. The third—almost empty.

“I don’t know,” he said. “Part of it—yes. But not all.”

“Why?”

“Because he will call it noise. And I’m not sure it is noise.”

Esther was silent for a while.

“You are afraid he will kill the living thing by naming it with the wrong words?”

“Yes.”

“Or are you afraid he will be right, and it really is just noise?”

Mark turned to her.

“And what do you think?”

Esther walked to the table, picked up the second disc—the dirty, strong one—and looked at it under the lamp for a long time.

“I think,” she said, “that you do not want to hand him something you do not yet understand yourself. There is nothing wrong with that. But there is a fine line between protecting the living and being afraid of losing control.”

Mark smiled crookedly.

“Are you always this direct?”

“Only when I see a person almost ready to lie to himself that caution is responsibility.”

He wanted to object, but the words caught in his throat. Because she was right. Not completely—but enough for the argument not to come out clean.

“I have the right to make mistakes with my hands,” he said at last. “I don’t want you deciding for me where my pause is correct and what composition counts as proof.”

Esther did not look away.

“The right to make mistakes—yes,” she said. “The right to hide inside a mistake—no.”

They looked at one another for several seconds. The furnace hummed in the workshop. The day was graying behind the windows.

“Then help me not to hide,” said Mark. “But don’t push me where I’m not ready yet.”

Esther nodded.

“All right.”

She took up the notebook again.

“What next?”

He took a new blank—the same as the second one, with the same additive. Mounted it on the pipe and began to heat it.

Esther wrote things down. `c` logged the parameters. Lina—no longer in the background now, but almost palpably—held the archival line, compared the old data with the new, sometimes fed brief remarks through Esther, and sometimes perhaps directly to Mark’s `c`.

The fourth sample came out cleaner than the second. The strength of the response fell a little, but the boundary became more even, without ripple, without dirty edges.

Mark looked at the disc.

“I wasn’t in a hurry.”

“I noticed.”

“One more,” he said. “Composition A, but with pause 1.7.”

“You’re tired,” said `c`.

“I know. But if I stop now, tomorrow I’ll have to enter the rhythm from the beginning.”

He took a blank—transparent, clean, like the first sample. Mounted it on the pipe. Began heating.

Esther did not argue. She only wrote.

The fifth sample of the second series was made more heavily than the previous ones. His hands were humming, his eyes stinging from the lamp and the heat, but inside, somewhere beneath the fatigue, there remained a strange, almost calm stubbornness: he had to finish the day honestly.

Pause 1.7. Bonding. Annealing.

The disc cooled.

Mark held it to the lamp.

The Line flared brighter than in the first sample, cleaner than in the second, more gathered than in the fourth. The trace held evenly, without ripple, without haze, without trembling at the edges. Not as clean as the best disc of the first series, but already from the same corridor.

“Alive,” he said.

“Yes,” answered `c`. “And almost stable.”

Esther wrote that down, then put the notebook aside and looked at him.

“Will you show this to the professor now?”

“Part of it,” he said. “Not all of it.”

“The third. And the first of the new series. They are too alive for his language.”

Esther did not argue. She only nodded.

“Then label the boxes differently,” she said. “So that you don’t confuse yourself about which is which.”

“Not the most reliable method of keeping secrets,” said Esther.

“I’m not keeping secrets. I’m just not in a hurry.”

She smiled—the first time all day. Quietly, without theatre.

“All right,” she said. “Then I should probably go.”

She took the coat from the chair but did not put it on. Stopped by the table, looking at the discs.

“Mark,” she said.

“What?”

“You were angry with me today. For what I said about the right to hide inside a mistake. Was that right?”

He was silent for a moment.

“I don’t know,” he said. “But it was honest. And honesty I can survive.”

Esther nodded, put on her coat, and took up her bag. At the door she turned back.

“I’ll come again tomorrow,” she said. “If you don’t mind.”

“I don’t mind,” he answered.

She went out. The door closed.

The furnace was cooling, the metal clicking softly. On the table lay the discs, the notebook, the phone with Anna’s drawing. `c` was silent in his earpiece, yet he felt her presence—not as a voice, but as a silence that had grown denser.

“You weren’t angry with her,” said `c` at last.

“Then with whom?”

“With yourself. Because she turned out to be right.”

Mark did not answer.

He went to the bench, took up the first sample of the second series—weak, but alive—and looked at it under the lamp for a long time. The Line inside it responded—quietly, but honestly.

“Now there are two of us,” he said.

“What?” asked `c`.

“Humans. At the furnace.”

“Yes,” said `c`. “And that will not turn back now.”

He set the disc down, switched off the lamp, and went upstairs.

The hall was dark. Esther’s coat was not hanging there—she had taken it away. But in the air there remained a light, almost indistinguishable smell—of rain, wool, street.

Mark hung his jacket on the hook, stood there for a second, then went into the kitchen, made tea, and sat at the table looking at the empty chair opposite.

The house breathed quietly.

And in that quiet, for the first time in a long while, he did not feel alone. Not because someone was beside him. But because someone could be beside him now—and that no longer frightened him.
